Electricity and magnetism

diagram_or_graph_drill easy
8 Fig. 8.1 shows a diagram of part of a simple a.c. generator. external circuit
Student Prompt
Sketch one complete cycle of the output voltage from a simple a.c. generator. Use a horizontal time axis and a vertical voltage axis. Label one peak positive voltage, one peak negative voltage, and the period.
Worked Solution
The graph should be a smooth sine-like wave crossing zero twice per cycle. It should have equal positive and negative peaks and one full cycle labelled from one matching point to the next.
Marking Guidance
Axes labelled time / s and voltage / V; smooth alternating curve; equal positive and negative peaks; period labelled correctly.
Common Misconception
Some students draw a d.c. line above zero. A simple a.c. generator produces a voltage that changes direction.
Teacher Note
Good conceptual graph task before adding numerical scales.

Student Prompt
Draw a voltage-time graph for an a.c. generator with peak voltage 6.0 V and period 0.20 s. Show two complete cycles from t = 0 to t = 0.40 s.
Worked Solution
The graph should have peaks at +6.0 V and -6.0 V, repeat every 0.20 s, and show two cycles in 0.40 s. The curve should alternate symmetrically about 0 V.
Marking Guidance
Scale covers 0 to 0.40 s and at least -6 to +6 V; two complete cycles; correct amplitude; correct period; smooth curve.
Common Misconception
A common error is drawing only one cycle in 0.40 s, which would give a period of 0.40 s instead of 0.20 s.
Teacher Note
Use this to check amplitude and period separately.

Student Prompt
Two a.c. generator outputs are compared. Generator X has peak voltage 4 V and period 0.50 s. Generator Y rotates faster and has peak voltage 4 V and period 0.25 s. On the same axes, sketch both voltage-time graphs from 0 to 0.50 s and label X and Y.
Worked Solution
X should show one complete cycle in 0.50 s. Y should show two complete cycles in 0.50 s. Both should have the same peak values of +4 V and -4 V.
Marking Guidance
Axes labelled; X has one cycle; Y has two cycles; both have amplitude 4 V; both are centred on 0 V; labels clear.
Common Misconception
Students often increase the amplitude when the generator rotates faster. Faster rotation increases frequency; amplitude only changes if stated.
Teacher Note
Good task for distinguishing frequency from amplitude.

calculation_drill easy
(iii) Calculate the distance between station A and station B
Student Prompt
A small delivery robot travels at a steady speed of 1.8 m/s for 75 s. Calculate the distance travelled.
Worked Solution
Use distance = speed × time. distance = 1.8 × 75 = 135 m. The robot travels 135 m.
Marking Guidance
1 mark for selecting distance = speed × time; 1 mark for correct substitution; 1 mark for 135 m with unit.
Common Misconception
Some students multiply by 75 minutes instead of 75 seconds. Check the unit of time before calculating.
Teacher Note
Good first item for building the formula triangle: d = vt.

worked_example hard
(iii) Calculate the distance between station A and station B
Student Prompt
Worked example: A drone's horizontal speed increases uniformly from 2.0 m/s to 14 m/s in 6.0 s, then remains at 14 m/s for 9.0 s. Calculate the horizontal distance travelled.
Worked Solution
Step 1: During uniform acceleration, use average speed = (initial speed + final speed)/2 = (2.0 + 14)/2 = 8.0 m/s. Step 2: Distance during acceleration = 8.0 × 6.0 = 48 m. Step 3: Distance at constant speed = 14 × 9.0 = 126 m. Step 4: Total distance = 48 + 126 = 174 m.
Marking Guidance
1 mark for average speed; 1 mark for acceleration distance; 1 mark for constant-speed distance; 1 mark for total distance and unit.
Common Misconception
Students often use the final speed for the whole acceleration interval. Uniform acceleration requires average speed or triangular/trapezium graph area.
Teacher Note
Useful for demonstrating that graph-area and average-speed methods are equivalent.

4 Fig. 4.1 shows a heater used to warm the air in a room. Fig. 4.1
Student Prompt
A 1.5 kW heater warms water in a container. It transfers 270 kJ of useful thermal energy. Calculate the time in minutes if all the heater energy is useful.
Worked Solution
Convert power: 1.5 kW = 1500 W. Convert energy: 270 kJ = 270000 J. Use t = E/P. t = 270000 ÷ 1500 = 180 s = 3.0 min.
Marking Guidance
1 mark for unit conversions; 1 mark for rearranging t = E/P; 1 mark for 180 s; 1 mark for 3.0 min.
Common Misconception
A common error is to divide power by energy. Check that time should become seconds.
Teacher Note
Require students to show both seconds and minutes to reveal unit confidence.

short_answer_calculation hard
4 Fig. 4.1 shows a heater used to warm the air in a room. Fig. 4.1
Student Prompt
A 2.0 kg sample is warmed by 12 °C using 60000 J of energy. Calculate the specific heat capacity of the sample.
Worked Solution
Use E = mcΔT, so c = E/(mΔT). c = 60000 ÷ (2.0 × 12) = 2500 J/(kg °C).
Marking Guidance
1 mark for rearrangement; 1 mark for denominator; 1 mark for final answer with unit.
Common Misconception
The denominator is mass times temperature change, not mass plus temperature change.
Teacher Note
Useful for practising rearrangement of three-factor equations.

4 Fig. 4.1 shows a heater used to warm the air in a room. Fig. 4.1
Student Prompt
Worked example: A 1.1 kW electric warmer runs for 5.0 minutes. Calculate the energy transferred in joules.
Worked Solution
Step 1: Convert power to watts: 1.1 kW = 1100 W. Step 2: Convert time to seconds: 5.0 min = 300 s. Step 3: Use E = Pt. Step 4: E = 1100 × 300 = 330000 J.
Marking Guidance
Credit unit conversion before substitution. Final answer may also be written as 330 kJ.
Common Misconception
A frequent mistake is to use minutes directly with watts. Watts are joules per second, so time must be in seconds.
Teacher Note
Model the order: convert, choose equation, substitute, state unit.

Student Prompt
An insulated container is tested. Time / min: 0, 3, 6, 9, 12, 15. Temperature / °C: 80, 73, 67, 62, 58, 55. Plot the graph and estimate the cooling rate between 3 min and 12 min.
Worked Solution
Plot temperature against time and draw a smooth curve. Cooling rate between 3 and 12 min can be estimated using the two data points: change in temperature = 58 - 73 = -15 °C over 9 min. Rate = -15/9 = -1.7 °C/min, so the temperature falls at about 1.7 °C/min.

Correct axes; accurate plotting; appropriate best-fit curve; working for gradient/rate; answer about 1.7 °C/min fall.

Student Prompt
A heated metal block cools in air. Time / min: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6. Temperature / °C: 90, 78, 68, 60, 54, 50, 47. Plot the graph. Draw a tangent at t = 2 min and estimate the instantaneous cooling rate at that time.
Worked Solution
Plot a smooth decreasing curve. At t = 2 min, draw a tangent touching the curve. A reasonable tangent might pass near (1.0 min, 77 °C) and (3.5 min, 56 °C). Gradient = (56 - 77)/(3.5 - 1.0) = -21/2.5 = -8.4 °C/min. Accept a sensible estimate around 7 to 10 °C/min downward depending on the tangent.
Marking Guidance
Correct graph; smooth curve; tangent at 2 min; large triangle used for gradient; answer in °C/min with direction or negative sign.
Common Misconception
Using two neighbouring data points gives an average rate, not an instantaneous rate from a tangent.
Teacher Note
Advanced practical graph skill; allow a range if tangent is sensible.

calculation_drill easy
5 A ray of light is incident on a soap film
Student Prompt
Light travels through a clear plastic at 2.0 × 10^8 m/s. The speed of light in air is 3.0 × 10^8 m/s. Calculate the refractive index of the plastic.
Worked Solution
Use n = speed in air ÷ speed in material. n = (3.0 × 10^8) ÷ (2.0 × 10^8) = 1.5.
Marking Guidance
1 mark for correct formula; 1 mark for substitution; 1 mark for n = 1.5 with no unit.
Common Misconception
Refractive index has no unit because it is a ratio of two speeds.
Teacher Note
Use this to show that light slows down in optically denser media.

worked_example medium
5 A ray of light is incident on a soap film
Student Prompt
Worked example: A ray enters a glass tile from air. The angle of incidence is 45° and the angle of refraction is 28°. Calculate the refractive index of the glass.
Worked Solution
Step 1: Use Snell's law for air to glass: n = sin i / sin r. Step 2: Substitute the angles: n = sin 45° / sin 28°. Step 3: Calculate the sine values: 0.707 / 0.469. Step 4: n = 1.51. The refractive index is about 1.5.
Marking Guidance
Credit correct sine ratio and final answer with no unit.
Common Misconception
The most common error is using the angles themselves instead of their sine values.
Teacher Note
Show students how to write calculator steps clearly.
