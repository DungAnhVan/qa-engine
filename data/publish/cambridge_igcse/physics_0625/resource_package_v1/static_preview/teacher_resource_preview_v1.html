Student Prompt
Sketch one complete cycle of the output voltage from a simple a.c. generator. Use a horizontal time axis and a vertical voltage axis. Label one peak positive voltage, one peak negative voltage, and the period.
Worked Solution
The graph should be a smooth sine-like wave crossing zero twice per cycle. It should have equal positive and negative peaks and one full cycle labelled from one matching point to the next.

Axes labelled time / s and voltage / V; smooth alternating curve; equal positive and negative peaks; period labelled correctly.

Some students draw a d.c. line above zero. A simple a.c. generator produces a voltage that changes direction.

Good conceptual graph task before adding numerical scales.

Scale covers 0 to 0.40 s and at least -6 to +6 V; two complete cycles; correct amplitude; correct period; smooth curve.

4 eye Fig. 3.1
Student Prompt
Plan an experiment to investigate how the length of a resistance wire affects the current in a circuit. You may use a low-voltage power supply, switch, ammeter, voltmeter, metre ruler, crocodile clips and resistance wire. Include the variables, method, table and graph.
Worked Solution
Set up a series circuit with the power supply, switch, ammeter and a measured length of resistance wire. Place a voltmeter across the wire and keep the supply voltage constant. Choose lengths such as 20, 40, 60, 80 and 100 cm. For each length, close the switch briefly, record current and voltage, then open the switch to reduce heating. Repeat readings and calculate mean current. Independent variable: length of wire. Dependent variable: current. Control variables: wire material, wire diameter, supply voltage and temperature as far as possible. Table columns: length / cm, current 1 / A, current 2 / A, mean current / A, voltage / V. Plot mean current against length or resistance against length if resistance is calculated.
Marking Guidance
MP1 suitable circuit with ammeter in series; MP2 voltmeter across wire or voltage kept constant; MP3 length varied over at least five values; MP4 current measured for each length; MP5 controls stated; MP6 repeats and mean; MP7 suitable table with units; MP8 graph named.
Common Misconception
Students may put the ammeter in parallel. An ammeter must be in series to measure current through the wire.
Teacher Note
Keep currents low and switch off between readings to reduce heating effects.

experiment_planning_task hard
4 eye Fig. 3.1
Student Prompt
Plan an experiment to investigate how the number of turns on a coil affects the strength of an electromagnet. You may use insulated copper wire, an iron nail, a low-voltage d.c. supply, switch, paper clips, ammeter and connecting leads. Include safety and reliability details.
Worked Solution
Wind insulated wire around an iron nail to make a coil. Connect the coil, switch, ammeter and low-voltage d.c. supply in series. Test numbers of turns such as 10, 20, 30, 40 and 50. For each trial, close the switch for the same short time and count the number of paper clips lifted. Open the switch after each trial to prevent heating. Keep the current as constant as possible by checking the ammeter and adjusting the supply if needed. Control variables include nail size/material, paper clip type, contact time, current and lifting method. Repeat each number of turns three times and calculate the mean number of clips. Table: turns, current / A, clips trial 1, clips trial 2, clips trial 3, mean clips. Plot mean number of clips against number of turns.
Marking Guidance
MP1 clear electromagnet setup; MP2 independent variable is number of turns; MP3 dependent variable measures strength; MP4 current controlled or monitored; MP5 other control variables; MP6 repeat readings; MP7 suitable table with units where needed; MP8 appropriate graph; MP9 safe low-voltage use and switch-off.
Common Misconception
A common weak plan changes both current and number of turns at the same time. Only one independent variable should be changed.
Teacher Note
Good extended planning item because students must operationalise 'strength'.

Student Prompt
A lamp is tested. Potential difference / V: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Current / A: 0, 0.18, 0.31, 0.41, 0.49, 0.55. Explain what happens to the resistance as potential difference increases.
Worked Solution
Calculate R = V/I at selected points. At 1 V, R = 1/0.18 = 5.6 Ω. At 5 V, R = 5/0.55 = 9.1 Ω. The resistance increases as potential difference increases. This is because the filament becomes hotter, making it harder for charge to flow.

Marks for calculating or comparing V/I, stating resistance increases, and giving heating explanation.
Common Misconception
A common error is to say resistance decreases because current still increases. Resistance depends on the ratio V/I, not just current.

(iii) Calculate the distance between station A and station B
Student Prompt
Worked example: A drone's horizontal speed increases uniformly from 2.0 m/s to 14 m/s in 6.0 s, then remains at 14 m/s for 9.0 s. Calculate the horizontal distance travelled.
Worked Solution
Step 1: During uniform acceleration, use average speed = (initial speed + final speed)/2 = (2.0 + 14)/2 = 8.0 m/s. Step 2: Distance during acceleration = 8.0 × 6.0 = 48 m. Step 3: Distance at constant speed = 14 × 9.0 = 126 m. Step 4: Total distance = 48 + 126 = 174 m.
Marking Guidance
1 mark for average speed; 1 mark for acceleration distance; 1 mark for constant-speed distance; 1 mark for total distance and unit.
Common Misconception
Students often use the final speed for the whole acceleration interval. Uniform acceleration requires average speed or triangular/trapezium graph area.
Teacher Note
Useful for demonstrating that graph-area and average-speed methods are equivalent.

Worked example: A ray enters a glass tile from air. The angle of incidence is 45° and the angle of refraction is 28°. Calculate the refractive index of the glass.
Worked Solution
Step 1: Use Snell's law for air to glass: n = sin i / sin r. Step 2: Substitute the angles: n = sin 45° / sin 28°. Step 3: Calculate the sine values: 0.707 / 0.469. Step 4: n = 1.51. The refractive index is about 1.5.

Student Prompt
A vibrating string is tested. Tension / N: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50. Wave speed / m/s: 18, 25, 31, 36, 40. Plot wave speed against tension. Decide whether speed is directly proportional to tension.
Worked Solution
The graph increases but is not a straight line through the origin. If speed were directly proportional to tension, doubling tension from 10 N to 20 N would double speed from 18 m/s to 36 m/s. It only increases to 25 m/s. Therefore speed is not directly proportional to tension.
Marking Guidance
Accurate plot; suitable scale; smooth increasing curve; comparison with direct proportionality; justified conclusion using data.
Common Misconception
Students often think any increasing graph means direct proportionality. Direct proportionality requires a straight line through the origin.
